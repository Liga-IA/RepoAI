## Laplace Smoothing

### The Zero Problem

During the training of Naive Bayes, it can happen that an attribute (like a word) never appears in a certain class. For example, imagine that in our email dataset, the word "promotion" appears in 50 emails of the spam class, but in **none** of the normal class emails.

**Training Data:**
* **Total Emails:** 3000 (with 900 being spam and 2100 normal).
* **Prior Probabilities:** $P(\text{spam}) = \frac{900}{3000} = 0.3$ and $P(\text{normal}) = \frac{2100}{3000} = 0.7$.
* **Occurrence of the Word "promotion":**
    * The word "promotion" appears 80 times in spam emails (out of a total of 50,000 words)
    * The word "promotion" appears 0 times in normal emails (out of a total of 120,000 words)

This results in the following probability calculation:

$P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{spam}) = \frac{80}{50000} \approx 0.0016$

$P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{normal}) = \frac{0}{120000} = 0$

The problem arises when a new email arrives containing the word "promotion" along with other words that could indicate it is a normal email. When using log-probabilities to classify, we will have:

$$\log P(\text{normal}) + \log P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{normal}) + \log P(\text{other words}|\text{normal})$$

Since $P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{normal}) = 0$, we have $\log(0) = -\infty$, and the entire score for the "normal" class results in $-\infty$. The algorithm will **never** classify an email as normal if it contains the word "promotion," no matter what the other words in the email suggest. This is a common problem that affects any Naive Bayes application [4].

### The Solution: Add-One Smoothing

The most common and elegant solution is **Laplace Smoothing**, also known as **Add-One Smoothing**. The idea is simple: before calculating the probabilities, we pretend that we have already seen each word in our vocabulary **one more time** in each class [2].

Instead of calculating the probability as a simple frequency:

$$P(X_i|C) = \frac{\text{Count of word } X_i \text{ in class } C}{\text{Total number of words in class } C}$$

We add `1` to the numerator and the vocabulary size to the denominator [3]:

$$P(X_i|C) = \frac{(\text{Count of word } X_i \text{ in class } C) + 1}{(\text{Total number of words in class } C) + |\text{vocabulary}|}$$

> [*NOTE*]
> **Why `+ |vocabulary|` in the denominator?**
>
> * If we add `1` "ghost occurrence" for the word $X_i$, to be fair and to keep the sum of probabilities equal to 1, we need to do this for **all** the words in the vocabulary. Since we have `|vocabulary|` words in total, the denominator increases by `|vocabulary|` (one extra count for each word) [3].

**Practical example with smoothing:**

$P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{spam}) = \frac{80 + 1}{50.000 + 10.000} = \frac{81}{60.000} = 0.00135$

$P(\text{"promotion"}|\text{normal}) = \frac{0 + 1}{120.000 + 10.000} = \frac{1}{130.000} \approx 0.0000077$

Now we can calculate with log-probabilities without problems:

* **SPAM:** $\log(0.3) + \log(0.00135) = -1.204 + (-6.608) = -7.812$
* **NORMAL:** $\log(0.7) + \log(0.0000077) = -0.357 + (-11.772) = -12.129$

Now other words in the email can influence the final classification; the model is no longer **locked into** an absolute decision.

Even if the word "promotion" has never appeared in normal emails, it is not reasonable to assume that its presence completely eliminates the possibility of an email being normal. Smoothing reflects this uncertainty: it assumes that even unobserved events can happen, just with low probability. This prevents the model from **completely ruling out** alternatives and becoming excessively rigid.

### Theoretical Basis: MLE vs MAP

The intuition behind smoothing has a solid theoretical foundation. To understand why it works, we can contrast two estimation approaches:

- **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE): "I only believe what I see in the data."**
    * This approach calculates probabilities by simply counting occurrences in the training data. Naive Bayes without smoothing uses this philosophy. Its formula is exactly what we used for the initial calculation, without correction [4]:
    
      $$P_{MLE}(X_i \mid C) = \frac{\text{Count of word } X_i \text{ in the class}}{\text{Total number of words in class } C}$$
    
    * The drawback is that if an event has never occurred in your sample, its probability is defined as zero, which makes the model fragile.

- **Maximum A Posteriori (MAP): "I combine what I already believe with what the data shows me."**
    * The fundamental idea of MAP is, in fact, to **incorporate "prior knowledge" (a priori) with the observed data** to arrive at a final estimate (a posteriori) [1].

    * In our context, **Laplace Smoothing** is a perfect example of a MAP estimate. The **+1** we add is a pseudocount. Our "prior knowledge" is the simple and useful assumption that every word in the vocabulary has a small chance of appearing, even if we haven't seen it in the training set. By adding a "ghost" count for each word, we are embedding this belief directly into our mathematical model.
