## Evaluation Metrics: Classification and Regression

The choice of metrics depends on the type of problem: **classification** or **regression**.

### Classification

In **classification** problems, the goal is to predict the correct class for each example. One possible way to evaluate performance is accuracy [1]:

#### Accuracy
It is the proportion of correct predictions.

$$
\
\text{Accuracy} = \frac{\text{Number of correct predictions}}{\text{Total number of examples}}
\
$$

### Regression Metrics

In **regression** problems, the goal is to predict continuous values. Among the most common metrics are the Mean Squared Error (MSE) and the Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$) [1]:

#### Mean Squared Error (MSE)
Measures the average squared error between the true and predicted values:

$$
\
\text{MSE} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2
\
$$

The lower the MSE, the better the model.

### Coefficient of Determination ($R^2$)
$R^2$ represents the proportion of variance explained by the model. [2]

To compute $R^2$, we use the formula taken from [2]:

$$
R^2 = \frac{TSS - RSS}{TSS}
$$

where:

- $TSS = \sum (y_i - \bar{y})^2$ is the **Total Sum of Squares**, which measures the total variability of the response variable $Y$ before applying regression. [2]
- $RSS = \sum (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$ is the **Residual Sum of Squares**, which measures the variability that remains **unexplained** by the model after regression has been performed. [2]

> [!NOTE]
> The difference $TSS - RSS$ represents the variability in the data that has been explained by the model.
> Therefore, the value of $R^2$ indicates what proportion of the total variability in $Y$ can be explained using the independent variables $X$. [2]

**Understanding the values of $R^2$:** [2]
- Values close to 1 indicate that the model explains a large portion of the data variability
- Values close to 0 indicate that the model does not explain much of the variability of the dependent variable.
